Leatherbound Pages

Leatherbound pages, covered in dust
Found in the depths of all that is lost and old
Flipped through by her prying hands
Tossed away by the very pair
And flown to the other end of the world
...perhaps they were forgotten

Leatherbound pages, clutched to her chest
In joy, confusion, and agony
Privy to the thoughts deep in her mind
More of a friend than a man could be
Tear-stained, ink-blotted, through wear and tear
But treasured, nonetheless

Leatherbound pages, how lonely they were
Though she sat right by their side
Staring, thinking, and maybe remembering
What could she write within?
Then alien gloves stole the pages away
And all she could do was watch

Leatherbound pages, long forgotten
Her words, fading away
Covers torn, sheets ripped, all covered in dirt
A full life, they had lived
But the pages refused to believe this
The pages still waited for her return


Description

This poem was the first that I had written on my own accord for a long time, and is still one of my favorites. It, like most of my poems, is NOT based on my own experiences/myself, and the "she"/"her" is simply a nondescript character.

The poem is supposed to be about passage through a person's life; in this case, the person is "her". The first verse is childhood, specifically a birthday. The "flurry of paper" refers to wrapping paper and the way the girl quickly forgot about the journal is common among children. The second verse takes place when the girl is leaving home, perhaps to college. She finds the journal again and puts it in the box that she is packing her belongings in. The third verse takes place when the girl is middle-aged. She finds the journal when she is undergoing hardships and it is a very useful friend to her. In the fourth verse, the girl is ill, and cannot write in the journal either because she is physically incapable or because she forgot how to. By the last verse, the journal has made its way to a junkyard. The girl is presumably dead and the journal is forgotten about.
